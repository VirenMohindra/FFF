Press Mentions

“Fusion embraces the idea that you can be bold, daring, funny, dark, witty, charming, spontaneous, etc. as a filmmaker regardless of your gender. This year’s festival is all about showcasing the ‘doers’ in the industry, like Desiree Akhavan.”

~NYU: Fusion Film Fest Marks New Season with Screening of Akhavan’s Appropriate Behavior 11/10/14

‘Can women save film criticism as an occupation? No, it’s beyond hope,’ said panelist Miriam Bale, who writes for the New York Times and other outlets. ‘It’s the only place I’ve seen that the rates get lower as you get more experience. But do I think women can save the culture of criticism? Maybe. I notice women whom I knew to be big film fans have this tendency to get really deep with what interests them. I think by opening that up, there is a chance to write different kinds of criticism than just reviews.‘ Bale also correlated having more female critics to challenging the problem of having few female directors, noting that more female voices could counter many male critics’ tendency to heroicize certain male directors.”

~ IndieWire: “Can Women Save Film Criticism — and Is It Worth Saving?” 3/11/14

Women behind the camera are rarely acknowledged in the film industry…The playing field remains uneven, yet women filmmakers are urgently working against the tide and producing great work. This is what the Fusion Film Festival at NYU is all about: shining a light on what is bubbling up from the new work all around us, and celebrating the work of women we deeply admire, who are giving us reasons to hope that we can find our way once we leave the sheltered world of a film school. Though every film in our competition is either shot by a woman or directed by a woman, Fusion doesn’t wave the banner of an exclusive girl’s club. The field evens out very quickly at Fusion. That’s what the real world should look like. That’s what we demand it looks like.“

~ IndieWire: “NYU’s Fusion FF: Celebrating Women in Film, Television, & New Media” 3/5/14

Film is not, or at least shouldn’t be, a battle of the sexes, and so Fusion opted to recognize films that featured a diversity in gender among their key crew rolls: female DPs working alongside male directors, male writers working closely with female directors… Gender collaborations, such as everyone from Mike Nichols and Elaine May, to Jason Reitman and Diablo Cody, to Mark Boal and Kathryn Bigelow have been responsible for some of the most important and memorable films in recent American history. The “why” is obvious; when you have diversity behind the camera, stories are told which can be universally related to. Ever since, Fusion, an entirely student run and operated endeavor with the ardent support of advisor Susan Sandler, has become one of the hallmarks of NYU’s academic year. Its film screenings and competitions have been paired alongside panels, talks, and amazing collaborations with the likes of Miranda July, Nancy Meyers, Amy Heckerling, Jane Campion, and Lena Dunham.“

~ IndieWire: “Fusion Film Festival at NYU Looks Back on the State of Gender Equality
in the Film Industry on its Tenth Anniversary” 3/7/13

Fittingly, last Friday night Cloud Atlas kicked off this year’s Fusion Film Festival, which serves to celebrate and encourage female filmmakers in the NYU community. The festival doesn’t really begin until March, but Cloud Atlas is such an appropriate selection for a launch event not just because of its statements about gender and identity, but because of what it says about art too.”

~ NYU Local: “Fusion Film Festival Kicks Off the Year With Miraculous Cloud Atlas” 10/26/12

Last Friday, as part of the nearly week-long celebration of women filmmakers, the Fusion Film Festival screened works by graduate and undergraduate students of Tisch. Featuring works from Tisch Asia and our campus (Regular Tisch? New York Tisch? Tisch Tisch?), the shorts featured a wide variety of stories and quality. Divided into the categories of Sight and Sound, Ungraduate Film, Music Video, and Graduate Film, prizes were given out in each section, along with an Audience Choice and overall award.”

~ NYU Local: “Fusion Festival Screens Student Works From New York and Tisch Asia” 3/7/2011

The Eighth Annual Fusion Film Festival kicked off yesterday in the spirit of NYU’s domination of the Oscar’s (Bragging about James Franco to non-NYU kids is still so satisfying, right?) The Fusion Film Festival, a student-run event, is an effort to encourage and recognize female achievement in filmmaking and promises free food, some free movies and a chance to pitch documentaries to HBO, A&E and IFC executives for $1000 in finishing funds. Oh, and Joan Jett of the The Runaways came to visit last year.”

~ NYU Local: “Celebrate Women and Movies at NYU’s Fusion Film Festival” 3/2/2011

For their seventh year, the Fusion Film Festival presented a special screening of The Runaways last night. At the last minute, Fusion announced that Joan Jett, the rock icon who started the Runaways, would be at the screening, and available for a Q&A session afterward. Joan, who arrived early (I thought all rock stars are supposed to come late), oozes cool. She’s in her early fifties, but she is still totally rocking. Dyed black hair, motorcycle boots, tattooed arms, and a voice warped from thousands of cigarettes — it was clear that everyone in the audience was blown away by her awesomeness… The Runaways is the perfect fit for the Fusion Film Festival, a movie about strong women written and directed by a woman, Floria Sigismondi. It’s a great tale of girl power and rock and roll; set in 1975, it’s pretty much an Almost Famous for girls. Kristen Stewart plays Joan Jett, a shy badass who starts the first big all-girl rock band.”

~ NYU Local: “Joan Jett Visits the Fusion Film Festival” 2/26/2010
